Private File Sharing: Why “Nothing Stored” Beats “Encrypted Storage”

Filetra Blog · July 2026

Every cloud service promises your files are "encrypted at rest." That sounds reassuring — until you notice what it admits: your file is resting on their server. The strongest privacy guarantee isn't better encryption of the stored copy. It's having no stored copy at all.

The problem with every stored copy

Once your file lands on a provider's server, its fate depends on things you can't see:

  • Retention policies. "Deleted" files often persist in backups for months.
  • Forgotten links. That share link from 2024 may still work today.
  • Breaches. A provider-side compromise exposes files from thousands of users at once.
  • Legal access. Stored data can be subpoenaed; data that was never stored cannot.
  • Account takeover. Anyone who gets into your cloud account gets your file history.

Encryption at rest mitigates some of these — if the keys are managed well, which you also can't verify. Zero storage eliminates the entire category.

How zero-storage sharing works

Peer-to-peer transfer over WebRTC streams the file directly between two browsers through an encrypted channel. The service in the middle only performs the introduction — like a switchboard operator connecting a call, then hanging up. During the transfer, the file exists in two places: your device and, progressively, the recipient's. Afterwards, still just those two. There is no third copy to leak, expire, or subpoena.

Ephemerality as a feature

A Filetra link is alive only while the sender's tab is open. This feels like a limitation until you reframe it: the link cannot outlive your intent to share. There is nothing to remember to delete, no "manage shared links" page to audit, no cleanup. Closing a tab is the most reliable revocation mechanism ever designed.

When you still want storage

Zero-storage sharing is for transferring a file, not hosting one. Team wikis, portfolios, and archives belong in storage you control. But the everyday act of getting a file from your machine to one other person — a contract, a video edit, a database dump — rarely needs a permanent server-side copy. It just gets one by default, because that's how the popular tools happen to work.

Takeaway

Ask one question before you share: does this file need to exist on a third server? If the answer is no, send it directly. It's free, encrypted end-to-end, and leaves nothing behind. Learn more about the security model or try it now.
