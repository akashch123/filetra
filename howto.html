How to Send Large Files for Free — Without Uploading to the Cloud

Filetra Blog · July 2026

You have a 12 GB video, a folder of RAW photos, or a huge project file — and every free tool you try hits a wall. WeTransfer caps free transfers at 2 GB. Email gives up at 25 MB. Google Drive wants you to eat into your storage quota and share a link that lives forever. There is a simpler way: skip the cloud completely and send the file directly from your browser to the recipient's browser.

Why most "free" file sharing tools hit limits

Traditional file sharing services work in two hops: you upload the file to their servers, then the recipient downloads it. Storing your file — even for a few days — costs the provider real money in disk space and bandwidth. That cost is exactly why free plans have size caps, expiry dates, download limits, and ads.

The alternative: peer-to-peer (P2P) transfer

Peer-to-peer file transfer removes the middle hop. Using a browser technology called WebRTC, your browser opens a direct, encrypted connection to the recipient's browser, and the file streams straight across. Because nothing is ever stored on a server:

  • There is no size limit. A 50 GB file works just as well as a 5 MB one.
  • It's genuinely free. No storage means no storage cost to pass on.
  • It's faster in most cases. The transfer runs at the speed of the slower of your two connections — one hop instead of an upload followed by a download.
  • Nothing is left behind. When you close the tab, the file is gone from the link. There is no forgotten copy sitting in a data center.

How to send a large file in 3 steps

  1. Open Filetra and drop your file onto the page. No account, no install.
  2. Copy the share link that appears and send it to your recipient over any channel — chat, email, SMS.
  3. Keep the tab open. The moment your recipient opens the link and accepts, the file streams directly to them with a live progress bar on both sides.

One thing to know

Because the file travels from your device in real time, both browser tabs need to stay open during the transfer. That's the trade-off for having no server in the middle — and it's also your privacy guarantee: the link dies the second you close the tab.

When P2P is the right choice

Direct transfer shines when the file is large, when privacy matters, or when you just want it done now without creating an account. If you need a link that works for a week while you're offline, a cloud service is the better fit. For everything else, sending it directly is simpler, faster, and free of limits.
