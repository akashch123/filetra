Is Peer-to-Peer File Sharing Safe? Security Explained Simply

Filetra Blog · July 2026

"Peer-to-peer" earned a shady reputation in the file-sharing wars of the 2000s. But the P2P used by modern browser file transfer has nothing to do with public torrent networks — it's a private, encrypted, one-to-one channel between two people who share a link. Here's what actually happens under the hood.

Every transfer is encrypted by default

Browser-to-browser transfers run on WebRTC, the same technology behind Google Meet and WhatsApp calls. Every WebRTC data channel is mandatorily encrypted with DTLS (the datagram version of TLS, the protocol securing HTTPS). This is not an optional setting — an unencrypted WebRTC connection is impossible by specification. Someone snooping on your network sees only ciphertext.

No server ever holds your file

With cloud sharing, you have to trust the provider's storage, their employees, their retention policy, and their breach history. With direct transfer there is nothing to breach: the file exists only on your device and the recipient's. A tiny signaling server helps the two browsers find each other — it exchanges connection metadata, never file contents.

The link is the key — treat it like one

Anyone who has the share link while your tab is open can request the file. Practical implications:

  • Send the link over a channel you trust (a direct message, not a public post).
  • The link works only while your tab is open — close it and the link is permanently dead.
  • There is no lingering copy for someone to find next month.

What P2P transfer doesn't protect against

Honesty matters in security. Direct transfer can't help if the recipient's device is compromised, and like most connections it exposes your IP address to the person you're transferring with — the same as any video call. If you need to hide your IP from your own recipient, you'd need a VPN on top.
